Content
I needed to refresh my soldering skills, since I was rusty with soldering without the use of Flux, and the last time I had soldered without it was five months ago, during my Co-Op.
I decided to watch this video, which showed me how to solder without flux, and how to properly tin the soldering iron. I also learned how to properly clean the soldering iron, which I had not done before.

Since I was still unsure of my abilities, I decided to take an old PCB and solder some header pins without flux to practice. I went to a solder station, heated up the iron, and got to work soldering.
One of the things I figured out quite quickly was that although the iron was hot enough, I needed to let the solder sit for a very exactly amount of time, or it would either group up or not set.
There was a method I began to employ, where I would heat the pad for exactly 2 seconds, put the solder and other things, and wait exactly 1 second, before removing the solder. This allowed me to consistently solder, with very little error.
An image of me soldering is found below:

Another thing I found quite quickly without flux was that the solder smoke would quickly rise. I needed to offset this by adding a fan pushing the smoke away from me at all times.
After I employed both these tactics and protected myself thoroughly through eye and mask protection (from the smoke), I was able to solder 16 pins onto a practice PCB, successfully learning how to solder without flux.
The prototype PCB I soldered is found below, with the 16 pins I soldered completely attached:

Another thing which I had to do after the practice PCB was complete was check for connectivity. This would make sure all the solder points actually attached where they were supposed to.
After testing all 16 points, they all beeped when I attached the multimeters' probes to them, so I knew that the soldering was successful.
While soldering, I noticed that the heat distribution on the pad was inconsistent, leading to uneven solder joints. To fix this, I adjusted the tip angle of the soldering iron to ensure full contact with the pad before applying solder. This significantly improved joint consistency.
Another challenge I faced was that some pins were not wicking solder properly. I realized this was due to oxidation on the header pins. To resolve this, I lightly sanded the pins with fine-grit sandpaper before soldering, which helped the solder flow better.
To further refine my technique, I experimented with different solder wire thicknesses. I found that using a thinner 0.6mm solder wire gave me better control over the amount of solder applied, preventing excessive buildup on the pads.
